Gastroenterologist Dr. B. Purevsuren

1. Proper meal management is the best treatment. Eat 3–5 small meals a day regularly. Limit fried, fatty, and spicy foods. Skipping meals is a major cause of stomach problems.
2. Drinking water is essential for digestion. Drink 1.5–2 liters of warm water daily. Avoid drinking large amounts immediately after meals; wait 30 minutes to help digestion.
3. Pay attention to stomach discomfort, nausea, or heartburn. Strong, frequent burning or regurgitation may indicate reflux. If medication only temporarily helps, consult a doctor. Avoid lying down immediately after dinner; eat lightly in the evening.
4. For bloating or constipation, combine proper diet, water, and exercise. Eat fresh vegetables, fruits, oatmeal, and grains daily. Limit coffee, soda, and sweets. Walking 20–30 minutes daily stimulates bowel movement.
5. Reducing alcohol protects the liver and stomach. Daily alcohol can cause fatty liver and gastritis. Avoid drinking alcohol before meals as it irritates the stomach.
6. Avoid self-medicating with liver supplements. “Liver vitamins” do not cure liver diseases. Some over-the-counter medicines can harm the liver.
7. Prevent intestinal infections and poisoning. Avoid spoiled or unknown origin foods. Wash hands regularly, especially after handling meat. Be cautious with street food for children.
8. See a doctor immediately if you experience severe abdominal pain, persistent heartburn, black or bloody stools, loss of appetite, unexplained weight loss, frequent vomiting, or jaundice.
9. Regular preventive check-ups are important. Liver function tests, Helicobacter tests, endoscopy of stomach and colon, and liver ultrasound help detect diseases early, maximizing treatment success.

ENT Specialist Dr. D. Mungundulguun

1. Do not use antibiotics for common cold or sore throat without guidance. Most colds are viral; unnecessary antibiotics weaken immunity and may cause resistance. See a doctor if fever persists.
2. Ear pain or hearing loss should not be ignored. Water entering the ear or nasal congestion can cause pain. Ringing or muffled hearing indicates potential hearing issues. Avoid inserting cotton swabs too deeply.
3. Treat nasal congestion and allergy symptoms promptly. Use nasal sprays regularly during spring allergies. Chronic nasal blockage can lead to breathing problems and sleep issues. Children with enlarged adenoids often have persistent congestion.
4. For throat inflammation, avoid spicy and cold foods. Drink warm fluids. Smoking and alcohol irritate the throat. Persistent throat lumps should be checked for tonsils or adenoids.
5. Special care for young children: Do not apply heat directly for ear pain as it may worsen inflammation. Loud noise is harmful to hearing. Mouth-breathing or snoring may indicate enlarged adenoids.
6. Prevent seasonal colds by washing hands regularly, keeping the head and neck warm in cold weather, and ensuring indoor humidity is adequate.
7. Smoking is the main enemy of ENT organs. It damages throat mucosa and causes chronic disease. Avoid smoking near children to prevent middle ear infections.
8. Get timely check-ups: hearing tests (audiogram), middle ear pressure measurement (tympanometry), and nasal/throat endoscopy. Early detection = faster recovery.

Radiology Specialist Dr. T. Mutash

1. Choosing the right test is crucial. X-ray, ultrasound, CT, and MRI serve different purposes. Follow your doctor's instructions. More images do not always mean better results; the right test ensures accurate diagnosis.
2. X-ray and CT involve radiation. Avoid frequent exposure if possible. Pregnant women should minimize radiologic tests. Children should receive the lowest possible radiation dose.
3. Understand the difference between MRI and CT. CT is fast and widely used for cardiovascular or emergency cases. MRI shows soft tissue, nerves, brain, spine, and joints more clearly. MRI is not suitable for people with metal implants (pacemakers, screws, etc.).
4. Ultrasound is safe. Commonly used for pregnancy monitoring and internal organ examinations. No radiation; safe for children and pregnant women.
5. How to prepare for tests: Abdominal ultrasound – fast for 6–8 hours. Bladder/kidney ultrasound – drink plenty of fluids and hold urine. For CT/MRI with contrast – inform the staff about allergies and kidney function.
6. For contrast-based tests: Inform staff if allergic to iodine, gadolinium, or seafood. Drink plenty of water after the test to help flush out the contrast agent.
7. Do not rush to interpret your images. Proper interpretation requires professional expertise. Correct diagnosis comes from the radiologist’s report combined with your doctor’s examination.
8. Staying still ensures high-quality images. Movement during CT/MRI can blur images and lead to misdiagnosis. Sedation may be needed for children.
9. Our goal is early disease detection. Preventive imaging, even without symptoms, protects against risk. Timely radiology exams detect many diseases early.

Clinical Laboratory Specialist Dr. Ts. Bayasgalan

1. Proper preparation is essential. Blood tests are usually done fasting (8–12 hours without food). Avoid coffee, tea, and sweets before tests. Always inform the lab of any medications you are taking.
2. Correct urine collection: First morning urine is the most reliable. Use a sterile container and collect midstream. Deliver the sample within 1–2 hours after collection.
3. Pediatric tests require calm conditions. Ensure children are well-rested, fed, and relaxed. Excessive fear or crying can affect test results.
4. Recommended situations for lab tests: Fatigue, weight loss, general weakness, fever, nausea, loss of appetite, unstable blood pressure, or children with growth or immune issues.
